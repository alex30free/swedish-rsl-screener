Strategy Overview

What is RSL?
The Levy Relative Strength (RSL) measures a stock's current price relative to its own 130-day moving average. A value above 1.0 means the stock trades above its 6-month average — a sign of sustained momentum.
How to use it
Buy the top-ranked stocks (highest RSL). Review and rebalance weekly. Sell any holding that drops out of the top tier or falls below its 130-day average (RSL < 1.0). Always combine with position sizing and risk management.
Why it works
Academic research confirms price momentum persists across 3–12 months. Stocks that have recently outperformed tend to continue outperforming — the RSL systematically identifies these leaders across the entire Swedish market.

What the Research Shows

R.A. Levy — "Relative Strength as a Criterion for Investment Selection", The Journal of Finance (1967)

01 · The Original Study

In his 1967 doctoral research, Robert Levy analysed 625 NYSE stocks over a 5-year period. He found that stocks with the highest relative strength — price divided by their 26-week (130 trading day) moving average — significantly outperformed the market in the subsequent period.

02 · The 130-Day Formula

Levy tested multiple lookback windows and found 26 weeks (130 trading days) to be the optimal period — long enough to capture a genuine trend, short enough to react when momentum shifts. Stocks with RSL above 1.0 are in an uptrend relative to their own history.

03 · Outperformance Results

Levy's top-ranked stocks by RSL produced excess annual returns of roughly 10–14% above the market average in his backtests. The strategy worked best when buying only those stocks with RSL clearly above 1.0 and rotating out when they dropped below.

04 · Why It Still Works

Subsequent academic research by Jegadeesh & Titman (1993) and others confirmed momentum effects persist across markets and decades. The core insight — winners keep winning over 3–12 month horizons — remains one of the most robust anomalies in finance.
